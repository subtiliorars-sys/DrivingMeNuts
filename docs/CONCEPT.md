# Driving Me Nuts — Concept

## Elevator pitch
A cozy pixel-art RPG + idle game where you run a roasted-peanut food truck and discover that real business is weirder and harder than you thought. Roast peanuts, drive around town, dodge the legume lecture, and learn actual small-business mechanics (margins, cash flow, seasonality, permits) without the spreadsheet.

## Target audience
- **Primary:** casual game players aged 13–45 (COPPA/GDPR-K threshold: 13+; see RISK_REGISTER CRIT-1) who love cozy games (Stardew Valley fans, comfort-gaming seekers)
- **Secondary:** educators, business students, small-business curious players who want to "learn by doing"
- **Tertiary:** idle-game enthusiasts (passive progression, prestige loops)

## Genre positioning
- **Core:** cozy pixel-art RPG (Stardew, A Short Hike aesthetic)
- **Mechanics blend:** RPG routing + idle tycoon (Game Dev Story, Idle Miner Tycoon progression but slower, respectful of time)
- **Tone:** comedy through systemics and character, not slapstick; educational without lecturing

## Design pillars

### 1. Comedy as mechanic
The canon joke—customers pedantic about legumes, owner exasperated—recurs **as a gameplay event and narrative spine.** Each encounter is a light RPG scenario: listen, reply (or escape), get paid, move on. The joke teaches: customer service is not always pleasant; time management beats perfect politeness. Legume-lecture encounters also hint at peanut allergen seriousness: the owner is upfront that everything on the truck contacts peanuts and warmly points them to a nearby allergen-safe cart — honest labeling, not a refusal.

### 2. Real small-business literacy
Game teaches:
- **COGS & margin:** raw peanut cost → roasting time/fuel → price point
- **Cash flow:** startup capital (truck + permits) before first sale; supplier payment cycles vs. customer payment
- **Location strategy:** different neighborhoods = different foot traffic, seasonal demand (summer fair, winter holiday market)
- **Permits & licensing:** a real-game gate, not a tutorial popup (owning the truck requires initial paperwork; each new vehicle/location requires renewal)
- **Pricing & demand elasticity:** charge too much, sales drop; too low, go broke

Mechanics reflect reality: you can't escape overhead. Success = steady profit, not fantasy wealth.

### 3. Cozy pixel charm with an edge
Pixel art at 32×32 sprites, readable silhouettes, warm palette (roasted-peanut browns, creams, market-stall yellows). NPCs have personality (recognizable walk cycles, repeating routes). The truck is the home base—lovingly detailed, breakable (repairs cost money), and personal. The joke is warm, not mean; characters are flawed and relatable, not caricatures.

### 4. Respect the player's time
- **Idle design:** roasting happens in the background; you can walk away and come back to finished product
- **No mandatory grinding:** progress is slow but steady; prestige loop (NG+) is optional
- **Clear feedback:** you see COGS, revenue, net profit; you understand why you won't get rich overnight

### 5. Accessible breadth
Players can engage at their level:
- **Pure roleplay:** ignore numbers, just talk to NPCs and deliver the story
- **Strategy-lite:** optimize price and location casually
- **Business sim:** deep-dive into forecasting, seasonality, upgrade ROI

## The canon joke: why it matters

**Load-bearing roles:**

1. **Identity:** "Driving Me Nuts" is the truck's name *and* the punchline. It is instantly memorable and pun-proof (works whether you get it or not).

2. **Recurring mechanic:** every zone/week has a few customers who lecture. Each interaction:
   - Teaches customer-service patience (you learn early: not all customers are rational)
   - Models honest labeling + warm referral (an allergic customer asks; the owner is upfront that everything on the truck contacts peanuts and respectfully points them to a nearby allergen-free cart — earns reputation and trust rather than making an impossible promise)
   - Builds character depth (an NPC's daughter keeps asking; the parent gets annoyed at you, but it's a story beat, not a failure)

3. **Marketing hook:** relatable, funny in an understandable way, and instantly communicates "this game is about peanuts." Free marketing in a crowded cozy-game space.

4. **Tone-setter:** the gag is warm and a little self-aware. It signals: this game doesn't take itself seriously, but it takes business seriously. No cynicism; affectionate exasperation.

## Success metrics

### Launch window (closed alpha/beta)
- Players can complete the intro (get truck, make first 10 sales, unlock one new location)
- Core loops are clear: roast → sell → upgrade → repeat
- One legume-lecture NPC is memorable and funny
- Business numbers feel plausible (you hit breakeven around day 30–40 with reasonable play)

### Post-launch
- Players reach prestige (NG+) after 10–20 hours of play
- Casual players report "it's relaxing" and "I learned something I didn't expect"
- Educator interest: 1–2 teachers use segments in econ/small-business units
- Zero reports of allergy insensitivity (peanut allergy treated as serious concern, not joke fodder)

## Scope guard
Initial build focuses on:
- Town with 3–4 distinct zones (residential, market, office park, park)
- 2 legume-lecture NPCs (recurring, memorable)
- Roasting, pricing, and location-strategy loops only (no cosmetics or full RPG dialogue tree yet)
- 8–10 upgrade paths (truck capacity, roaster efficiency, quality tier, transport range)

Later phases add campaign narrative, employee hiring, seasonal events, cosmetics.
